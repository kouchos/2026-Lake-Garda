Peschiera sits in the middle of one of Italy's great wine corners. Within a short drive (or cycle) are the vineyards of Lugana, Bardolino, Custoza, Soave and the legendary Valpolicella (home of Amarone). This page is the grown-ups' guide — what to drink, what to bring home, and where to taste it. For the wider food scene see Food & Drink; for restaurants and what to order, see Dining.

1. Lugana — the local hero (white)

  • What it is: A crisp, mineral, gently floral dry white grown on the clay soils right here on the southern shore — the vineyards literally start on the edge of Peschiera and run west to Sirmione. Made from the Turbiana grape.
  • Why drink it: The definitive Lake Garda lunch wine — fresh, easy and low-fuss. If you order one white all holiday, make it this.
  • Ask for: a Lugana by the glass ("un calice di Lugana"); a bottle of Lugana Superiore if you want a touch more depth.
  • Pairs with: lake fish, spaghetti alle vongole, fried zucchini flowers, or just an afternoon by the water.

2. Bardolino & Chiaretto — the easy reds & rosé

  • What it is: Light, bright reds from the hills on the eastern shore (the town of Bardolino Map is ~20 min by car/ferry). The rosé version, Chiaretto, is pale, dry and summery.
  • Why drink it: Bardolino is one of the rare reds you can serve slightly chilled — perfect in 30°C heat and light enough for lunch.
  • Ask for: Bardolino (red) or Bardolino Chiaretto (rosé). A Bardolino Superiore is a small step up.
  • Pairs with: pizza, tomato-based pasta, grilled sausages — proper holiday food.

3. Bianco di Custoza & Soave — the friendly whites

  • Bianco di Custoza: An aromatic dry white from the Custoza Map hills just south of the lake (very close to Peschiera). Soft, floral and great value.
  • Soave: A smooth, almond-y dry white from east of Verona. Internationally famous and a safe crowd-pleaser.
  • Pairs with: antipasti, risotto, chicken and lighter dishes.

4. Valpolicella & Amarone — the serious reds

  • Valpolicella Classico: An easy, cherry-fruited everyday red.
  • Valpolicella Ripasso: Re-fermented on Amarone grape skins for extra body and warmth — the "baby Amarone", and superb value.
  • Amarone della Valpolicella: The king of Verona wines — rich, velvety and high in alcohol, made from grapes dried for months before pressing. A proper after-dinner treat; sip it slowly. Look for it on the list at Raffilù and other smarter spots in town.
  • Recioto della Valpolicella: The sweet ancestor of Amarone — a dessert red that's wonderful with chocolate.
  • Note: the Valpolicella vineyards are north of Verona (~40–50 min drive), so these are wines to order in restaurants rather than cycle to.

5. Prosecco & the Spritz culture

  • Prosecco: The Veneto's famous sparkling white — served as an aperitivo and as the base of a Spritz.
  • Aperol / Select Spritz: Prosecco + Aperol (or the Venetian Select) + soda and an orange slice — the 6pm drink by the lake. Order one and watch the sunset.
  • Hugo Spritz: Prosecco, elderflower and mint — lighter and very refreshing in the heat.
  • See the Food & Drink page for the full aperitivo rundown.

🍇 Tasting & Buying Near Peschiera

Distances are approximate from Del Garda Village — verify and book tastings ahead, especially in peak summer. We have no rental car (private transfer only), so the nearest Lugana cellars are the realistic "visit" option; everything else is best enjoyed by the glass in town.

  • Cantina Le Morette Map — a respected Lugana producer on the edge of Peschiera itself. The closest "proper winery" to the resort. (Cycle/taxi; confirm tasting hours.)
  • Zenato Winery Map — one of the most famous Lugana names, in San Benedetto di Lugana just west of town. Tastings by appointment.
  • Ca' dei Frati Map — the benchmark Lugana estate, near Sirmione (~15 min drive). Lovely tasting room.
  • Cantina di Custoza Map — a large, welcoming co-operative for Custoza and Bardolino; easy place to buy by the case.
  • Corte Gardoni Map — a friendly family estate near Valeggio sul Mincio (good to combine with a Borghetto / Mincio day) doing Bardolino, Chiaretto and Custoza.
  • In town: Peschiera's old town has enoteche (wine bars/shops) where you can taste a flight and buy bottles without leaving the lakefront — the no-driving, kid-in-tow friendly option.

🧳 What to Bring Home

  • A bottle of Lugana — the taste of this exact corner of the lake; a great-value souvenir (often €8–15 at the cellar door).
  • A Ripasso or Amarone — the "special bottle" for a winter dinner back home (Ripasso ~€12–20; Amarone €25+ and worth it).
  • Aer Lingus note: wine goes in checked baggage only (the 100ml liquid rule blocks full bottles in hand luggage). Wrap bottles in clothes or buy a padded wine sleeve; check the current checked-bag weight allowance before loading up. Confirm the duty-free / allowance details near the trip — see Packing.

📖 Reading an Italian Wine Label

  • DOC / DOCG — quality classifications; DOCG is the top tier (Amarone is DOCG).
  • Secco = dry · Amabile / Dolce = off-dry / sweet.
  • Superiore = a notch up in ripeness/ageing · Riserva = aged longer.
  • Classico = from the historic heartland of the zone.
  • Vendemmia = vintage (harvest year) · Cantina = winery/cellar.
  • More ordering words are on the Phrases page.

👨‍👩‍👧‍👧 For the Kids & Non-Drinkers

  • Most cantine and enoteche are happy to serve grape juice (succo d'uva) or soft drinks, and many estates have gardens for the children to potter about while you taste.
  • For the alcohol-free aperitivo hour, order the kids a Crodino or Chinotto so they can clink glasses too — see Food & Drink.
  • Don't drink and drive: Italy's limit is low (0.5 g/l, and zero for newer drivers). With our private transfer and bikes, it's easiest to taste in town or split a bottle over dinner.
