The boring-but-vital page: who to call, where the nearest pharmacy and hospital are, how your phone and money work in Italy, and the few safety notes worth knowing for a lakeside family holiday. Skim it once before you travel.

Emergency Numbers

112 is the single Europe-wide emergency number — it connects you to police, ambulance or fire, with English-speaking operators. Use it for anything urgent.

Health & Medical

  • Pharmacy (Farmacia): look for the green cross. There are several in Peschiera del Garda Map. Pharmacists handle minor ailments and can advise before you bother a doctor.
  • Out of hours: each town posts the farmacia di turno (duty pharmacy) on the door for nights/Sundays.
  • Nearest A&E (Pronto Soccorso): the closest hospitals with an emergency department are around Bussolengo and Villafranca di Verona (~20–30 min by car) Map. For anything serious, call 112/118 — they direct you. (Verify nearest open ER on arrival.)
  • Tourist doctor: in summer a Guardia Medica Turistica (tourist medical service) often operates near the resorts — reception will know.
  • GHIC/EHIC: your Irish/UK card gives access to state healthcare on the same terms as locals (bring it — it’s on the packing list). It is not a substitute for travel insurance, which covers private care, repatriation and the kids’ mishaps.
  • Tap water is safe to drink; refill bottles at public fountains in Peschiera, Verona and Venice.

Phone & Internet

  • Roaming: Ireland and Italy are both in the EU, so “roam like at home” applies — your Irish SIM uses your normal allowance at no extra cost. You do not need an Italian SIM or eSIM. Just check your plan’s fair-use data cap before streaming all day.
  • WiFi: free throughout Del Garda Village (see Resort).
  • Save offline: your Aer Lingus boarding passes and the Ziptransfers confirmation, plus an offline Google Maps area for Lake Garda — handy if signal drops.

Money

  • Currency: the euro (€). Cards (incl. contactless and Apple/Google Pay) are widely accepted, but carry some cash for markets, gelato, the restaurant coperto, beach kiosks and small bars.
  • Cash machines are labelled Bancomat; there are several in Peschiera Map. Decline the machine’s currency-conversion offer (choose to be charged in euro).
  • Cash you’ll need on arrival: the accommodation balance (€1,627.87), a refundable €100 security deposit, and the tourist tax — see Booking. Check with reception which of these can go on a card.
  • Tell your bank you’re travelling so cards aren’t blocked.

Documents & If Things Go Wrong

  • Keep passports, EHIC/GHIC, insurance details and booking refs together; photograph them all to your phone (and email a copy to yourself).
  • Lost passport / consular help: the Embassy of Ireland is in Rome Map — note its number before you travel; report a lost passport to the local police (Carabinieri) for a report first.
  • Have your travel-insurance emergency line saved — call it before paying for any private treatment.

Safety Quick-Reference

  • Sun & heat: high-SPF cream, hats and water for everyone; midday shade for the youngest (see Packing).
  • Storms: afternoon thunderstorms blow up fast — off the water and out of exposed spots if the sky darkens.
  • Lake swimming: shores shelve quickly and there are no lifeguards at most spots — keep the children close and use armbands; water shoes for the pebbles.
  • Boats: children’s life jackets are mandatory and provided — insist on a good fit (see Boat Hire).
  • In a crowd (Venice, Gardaland), agree a meeting point and put a card with a parent’s phone number in each child’s pocket.
